## Introduction

The continuous residence rule is one of the most misunderstood parts of ILR applications. Many people assume they just need to stay under a total number of days away across 5 years. The actual rule is more precise: each individual rolling 12-month window must stay under 180 days. This matters because you can be under the limit overall but still break the rule if absences cluster in one period. ## What Is Continuous Residence?

Continuous residence means that throughout your qualifying period — usually 5 years — the UK has been your primary home and you have lived here lawfully and without excessive interruption.

It does not mean you cannot leave. You can travel for holidays, work trips, family visits, and other reasons. The key requirement is that your absences stay within the legal limit.

Continuous residence is broken if you:
- Spend more than 180 days outside the UK in any rolling 12-month window
- Let your visa expire while abroad (leaving the UK without valid leave to return)
- Are imprisoned in the UK or abroad
- Leave the UK with no intention of returning

If continuous residence is broken, you typically need to restart the qualifying period from scratch.

## The 180-Day Rule: How It Actually Works

The 180-day rule applies to rolling 12-month windows. This is the part most people get wrong.

A rolling window is not fixed to a calendar year. It moves forward day by day. For any given date during your qualifying period, UKVI looks back 12 months from that date and counts how many days you were outside the UK in that window.

**Example:**

Imagine you were abroad for 100 days from October to January, and then abroad again for 90 days the following summer (May to August). That looks like 190 days total in roughly 10 months. At some point in that period, a rolling 12-month window will capture more than 180 days of absence. This breaks continuous residence — even though your total absences over the full qualifying period might look reasonable.

**This is why the total-days approach is not enough.** You need to check every rolling window, not just the overall count.

## What Counts as an Absence?

An absence is any day (or part of a day, depending on how UKVI counts it) spent outside the UK. This includes:

- Holiday travel
- Business trips
- Visiting family abroad
- Medical treatment abroad
- Time spent studying or working abroad

It does not include days spent in the UK even if you are a national of another country.

The general rule for counting days is that the day you leave and the day you return do not both count as full days of absence — typically the day of departure counts as a day in the UK and the day of return counts as a day outside. Check current Home Office guidance on GOV.UK for the precise counting method — this can affect borderline cases.

## What Breaks Continuous Residence?

There are three main ways to break continuous residence:

**1. Exceeding 180 days in a rolling 12-month window**

This is the most common issue. Going over 180 days in any window — even by one day — breaks continuous residence. UKVI checks all windows across the qualifying period, not just the most recent year.

**2. Leaving without valid leave to return**

If your visa expires while you are outside the UK, you have broken continuous residence. You would need to apply for re-entry — and this gap in lawful status means you cannot count that period toward ILR.

Always check your visa expiry date before travelling. If your visa expires soon, either extend it before you travel or return before the expiry date.

**3. Imprisonment**

A custodial sentence (prison) — whether in the UK or abroad — breaks continuous residence. Time spent in prison does not count toward the qualifying period.

---

## What Does Not Break Continuous Residence

**Short absences within the 180-day limit**

Any absence, no matter how frequent, is fine as long as no rolling 12-month window exceeds 180 days.

**Crown service**

If you work for the UK government abroad — in the diplomatic service, armed forces, or a similar role — those absences do not count against the 180-day limit. This is a specific exemption in the immigration rules.

**Permitted paid employment abroad**

Depending on your visa route, some work abroad may be permitted and treated differently. Check the rules for your specific visa if this applies to you.

## When Does the Qualifying Period Start?

The qualifying period usually starts on the date you first entered the UK on the relevant visa. If you were already in the UK when your visa was granted, the qualifying period starts from the date of the visa grant.

For most people on a Skilled Worker visa or similar route, this is the date you arrived in the UK on that visa. Previous visas (such as a student visa) do not count toward the ILR qualifying period.

## ILR Continuous Residence vs Citizenship Continuous Residence

These are different rules with different limits. Do not confuse them.

**ILR:** No more than 180 days in any rolling 12-month window during the 5-year qualifying period.

**British citizenship (after ILR):** No more than 450 days outside the UK in the full 5-year period, and no more than 90 days in the 12 months immediately before applying for citizenship.

You can technically meet the ILR rule (no single window over 180 days) but still fail the citizenship rule (too many total days over 5 years). If you are planning for citizenship, check both sets of rules from the start.

## How UKVI Checks Your Absences

UKVI has access to UK border records. They can see when you entered and left the UK through passport scans at the border. Do not estimate or guess your travel history in the application — UKVI can check.

Gather your travel records from:
- Your passports (including expired passports — keep them)
- Boarding pass records or airline booking confirmations
- Bank statements showing foreign transactions
- Travel insurance records

List every trip in your application, no matter how short. Unexplained discrepancies between your declaration and border records are treated seriously and can result in refusal on good character grounds.

## Common Mistakes

❌ **Calculating total days instead of rolling windows.** You can be under 180 days total but still break the rule if absences cluster. Always use a rolling-window calculator.

❌ **Travelling on an almost-expired visa.** If you leave the UK and your visa expires before you return, you have broken continuous residence. Check expiry dates before every trip.

❌ **Forgetting short trips.** A 3-day business trip still counts. Every absence must be recorded. UKVI's border data will show trips you forget to mention.

❌ **Assuming the rule resets at the calendar year.** The 12-month window rolls daily, not annually. There is no fixed reset point.

## Expert Tips

1. **Run the [absence calculator](/absence-calculator) before every trip, not just at application time.** Catching a problem early means you can adjust travel plans. Catching it during the application means it may be too late.

2. **Keep all passports, including expired ones.** Your old passport contains entry and exit stamps that prove your travel history. You will need them for the ILR application.

3. **Note the dates you enter and exit the UK every time you travel.** A simple spreadsheet with departure date, return date, and destination is enough. Do this from the start of your qualifying period.

4. **If you are close to 180 days in a window, delay your next trip.** Once the earliest date in the window drops off (12 months later), the window resets. A short delay can bring you back under the limit.

5. **Crown servants: get written confirmation of your posting.** If your absences were due to UK government service, get a letter from your employer confirming the dates and nature of the posting. Include it with your ILR application.

## FAQs

### What is the 180-day rule for ILR?

The 180-day rule means you cannot spend more than 180 days outside the UK in any rolling 12-month period during your qualifying period. Breaking this rule breaks continuous residence and can lead to ILR refusal.

### Does the 180-day window reset every year?

No. The window is rolling, not fixed to a calendar year. For any given date, UKVI looks back exactly 12 months. There is no annual reset point — the window moves forward day by day.

### What happens if I exceed 180 days in one rolling year?

Exceeding 180 days in any rolling window breaks continuous residence. This typically means you cannot use that period toward ILR and may need to restart the qualifying period from when you re-established lawful continuous residence.

### Can I still take holidays abroad?

Yes. You can travel freely as long as no rolling 12-month window exceeds 180 days. Many people take 2–3 holidays a year within this limit without any problem.

### Does time spent abroad for work count against the 180-day limit?

Yes, unless your work abroad is Crown service (working for the UK government). Standard overseas business trips count as absences.

### How do I check my absences before applying for ILR?

Use our [absence calculator](/absence-calculator) and enter each trip from your qualifying period. The tool checks every rolling 12-month window and flags any periods where you exceeded the limit.

### Is the continuous residence rule the same for all visa routes?

The 180-day rolling limit applies to most routes. Some routes may have additional or different rules. Always check the specific rules for your visa category in the current immigration rules.
