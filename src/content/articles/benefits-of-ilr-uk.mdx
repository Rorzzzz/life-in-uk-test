Indefinite Leave to Remain gives you the right to live and work in the UK permanently with no visa conditions, no sponsor dependency, and no renewal deadlines. You also stop paying the Immigration Health Surcharge, gain access to public funds, and start the 12-month countdown to British citizenship. The benefits are significant — but ILR is not the same as citizenship, and some limits remain.

Getting ILR is one of the most significant milestones in the UK immigration process. After years of visa conditions, renewals, and restrictions, ILR removes most of them in one step.

Read the full comparison at [ILR vs British citizenship](/articles/ilr-vs-british-citizenship) to understand what ILR gives you — and what only citizenship adds.

---

## Benefit 1 — Live and Work in the UK Permanently

ILR has no expiry date. You do not need to renew it, apply for an extension, or maintain any visa conditions. You can live in the UK indefinitely.

You can work for any employer in any sector. There is no Skilled Worker sponsor required. You can change jobs, take a gap year, become self-employed, or start a business without any immigration implications.

This is the most significant practical change from a work visa. On a Skilled Worker visa, losing your job means losing your visa. On ILR, your right to stay does not depend on employment.

---

## Benefit 2 — No More Immigration Health Surcharge

On a work or family visa, you pay the Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) upfront when applying. From April 2026, the IHS is £1,035 per year — so a 3-year visa costs £3,105 in IHS alone per person.

Once ILR is granted, you stop paying the IHS. You are treated as an ordinary UK resident for NHS purposes — the same as someone born here.

Over the years between ILR and citizenship, this saves thousands. A family of four on ILR saves over £12,000 in IHS over three years compared to remaining on visas.

---

## Benefit 3 — Access to Public Funds

Most visa holders in the UK have a condition called "No Recourse to Public Funds" (NRPF). This means they cannot claim most welfare benefits — including Universal Credit, Housing Benefit, and Child Benefit.

ILR removes NRPF. As an ILR holder, you can access most public funds, subject to the same eligibility tests as UK nationals (including the habitual residence test for some benefits).

This matters most if circumstances change unexpectedly — redundancy, illness, family breakdown. ILR means you have access to the safety net that was not available on a visa.

---

## Benefit 4 — NHS as an Ordinary Resident

ILR holders are treated as ordinary residents for NHS purposes. This means:

- GP registration with no restrictions
- Hospital treatment at no charge (same as UK nationals)
- No requirement to pay overseas visitor charges
- No IHS to renew

You already had NHS access on most work and family visas, but you were paying the IHS for that access. ILR removes that cost while maintaining the same rights.

---

## Benefit 5 — Greater Mortgage and Rental Security

Many UK lenders and landlords treat visa holders differently from settled residents. Mortgages with visa expiry within the loan term can lead to higher rates, additional checks, or refusals.

ILR gives lenders and landlords confidence that your UK residence is permanent. Many ILR holders find that mortgage applications become easier and rental applications face fewer challenges.

---

## Benefit 6 — Sponsor Family Members

ILR holders can sponsor certain family members to join them in the UK. This includes spouses, civil partners, and dependent children.

The rules are complex and fees are significant — but the right to sponsor family is a meaningful benefit that visa holders do not always have. Use the [ILR document checklist](/ilr-checklist) to understand the supporting documents required for family sponsorship applications.

---

## Benefit 7 — No Sponsor Dependency

On a Skilled Worker visa, your right to remain in the UK is linked to your employer. If your employer loses their sponsor licence, your visa is at risk. If you are made redundant, you have a 60-day grace period to find new sponsorship.

ILR removes this entirely. Your right to remain is yours — it does not depend on any employer, any licence, or any third party.

---

## Benefit 8 — Pathway to British Citizenship

ILR is the required step before applying for British citizenship by naturalisation. Once you have held ILR for 12 months, you can apply.

Citizenship gives you:
- A British passport — with visa-free or visa-on-arrival access to 190+ countries
- The right to pass British citizenship to children born outside the UK
- A permanent and irrevocable right to remain — citizenship cannot lapse like ILR

See the [requirements for British citizenship](/articles/british-citizenship-requirements-2026) for the full checklist.

---

## Benefit 9 — Protection Against Future Policy Changes

UK immigration rules change regularly. Visa routes are added, modified, and removed. The government has proposed extending the ILR qualifying period from 5 to 10 years for new applicants.

If that change is passed, applicants who have already secured ILR would not be affected. Getting ILR now, while the 5-year route remains open, protects you against future rule changes that could make the process significantly harder.

---

## Benefit 10 — Peace of Mind

The intangible benefit that ILR holders consistently report is the end of uncertainty.

No more calendar alerts for visa renewal deadlines. No more checking whether a job change affects your visa conditions. No more calculating whether a holiday puts you over the absence limit for your current visa.

ILR does not eliminate all immigration considerations — the 2-year absence rule still applies, and you are not yet a British citizen — but it removes the constant low-level stress that visa conditions create.

## What ILR Does NOT Give You

ILR is not citizenship. There are things citizenship gives you that ILR does not:

- **No UK passport.** You still travel on your national passport. Your entry rights to other countries are determined by your nationality, not your ILR.
- **ILR can lapse.** If you spend 2 or more continuous years outside the UK, your ILR is lost. British citizenship cannot be lost this way.
- **Children born abroad.** Children born outside the UK after you hold ILR do not automatically become British citizens. With citizenship, children born abroad may be eligible for British nationality.
- **No right to vote in general elections.** Only British citizens can vote in UK general elections. ILR holders can vote in local elections in some circumstances.

## Common Mistakes

❌ **Thinking ILR never expires under any circumstances**
ILR has no expiry date, but it lapses automatically if you leave the UK for 2 or more continuous years. Many people are unaware of this rule. If you plan extended periods abroad, check the rules carefully. See [does ILR expire](/articles/does-ilr-expire) for the full details.

❌ **Assuming access to public funds begins immediately**
NRPF is removed when ILR is granted, but some benefits require passing the habitual residence test. Universal Credit, for example, requires you to show you are habitually resident in the Common Travel Area. This is usually straightforward for long-term UK residents but can affect recent arrivals.

❌ **Thinking ILR gives the same travel rights as a British passport**
ILR gives you the right to live in the UK. For international travel, you use your national passport. Your entry rights to other countries depend on your nationality — not your UK immigration status.

❌ **Applying for citizenship before the 12-month ILR wait is complete**
You must hold ILR for 12 months before applying for naturalisation. Applications submitted too early are refused and the £1,839 fee is lost. The exception is spouses of British citizens — they can apply for citizenship immediately after ILR. See [requirements for British citizenship](/articles/british-citizenship-requirements-2026).

## Expert Tips

**1. Time your ILR application for when all conditions are met.** Every month you delay after eligibility is a month of paying the IHS unnecessarily and a month further from citizenship. There is no benefit to waiting.

**2. Start the 12-month citizenship clock immediately.** The day your ILR is granted starts the 12-month countdown to citizenship eligibility. Keep a record of the exact grant date — you will need it for the citizenship application.

**3. Keep travelling within the 2-year rule.** ILR holders can travel freely — but no single trip should exceed 2 years. If you plan extended international work or travel, take legal advice before going. Losing ILR means reapplying from scratch.

**4. The IHS saving is real money.** If you were paying IHS on your visa, calculate what you were paying annually. That saving starts the moment ILR is granted and continues every year until you die or leave the UK permanently.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the main benefits of ILR in the UK?

ILR gives you permanent residence with no visa conditions, no sponsor requirement, and no renewal deadline. You stop paying the Immigration Health Surcharge, gain access to public funds, and can apply for British citizenship 12 months later.

### Does ILR give you the right to claim benefits?

Yes. ILR removes the No Recourse to Public Funds condition. You can access most welfare benefits, subject to the same eligibility tests as UK nationals, including the habitual residence test for some benefits.

### Does ILR ever expire?

ILR has no expiry date but lapses automatically if you leave the UK for 2 or more continuous years. There is no renewal deadline, but you must maintain continuous residence to keep ILR active.

### Can I get a British passport with ILR?

No. ILR gives you the right to live in the UK permanently, but not a British passport. To get a British passport, you must apply for naturalisation as a British citizen — which requires ILR plus 12 months of settlement.

### How long after ILR can I apply for British citizenship?

You can apply for British citizenship 12 months after your ILR is granted, provided you meet all other requirements — 5 years of legal residence, Life in the UK test pass, B1 English, and good character.
