Proportional representation (PR) is a voting system where each party receives seats in proportion to its share of the total votes. If a party wins 30% of votes, it gets roughly 30% of seats. The UK does not use PR for general elections — it uses First Past the Post. PR is used in some devolved elections in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

## What Is First Past the Post?

First Past the Post (FPTP) is the voting system used for UK general elections. In FPTP:

1. The UK is divided into 650 constituencies
2. Each voter chooses one candidate
3. The candidate with the most votes in that constituency wins — even if they do not have a majority
4. The party that wins the most constituencies forms the government

FPTP is simple. One vote, one winner per constituency. However, a party can win a large majority of seats in Parliament with far less than 50% of all votes cast nationally.

---

## How Proportional Representation Works

Under proportional representation, the number of seats a party wins reflects its share of the total vote.

**Example:**
- Party A wins 40% of votes → gets approximately 40% of seats
- Party B wins 30% of votes → gets approximately 30% of seats
- Party C wins 20% of votes → gets approximately 20% of seats

Under FPTP, Party C might win very few seats despite 20% of votes if their support is spread evenly across many constituencies rather than concentrated in particular areas.

## Where PR Is Used in the UK

The UK uses proportional or semi-proportional systems for devolved elections:

**Scottish Parliament:**
Uses the Additional Member System. Voters cast two votes — one for a constituency representative (FPTP) and one for a regional party list (PR). This produces a more proportional overall result.

**Welsh Senedd:**
Uses the same Additional Member System as Scotland.

**Northern Ireland Assembly:**
Uses Single Transferable Vote (STV). Voters rank candidates in order of preference. Seats are allocated to reflect those preferences proportionally.

**London Assembly:**
Uses the Additional Member System.

## Common Mistakes

❌ **Thinking the UK uses proportional representation for general elections**
The UK general election uses First Past the Post. Proportional systems are used in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland for devolved elections — not at Westminster.

❌ **Confusing Additional Member System with pure proportional representation**
The Additional Member System is a mixed system — partly FPTP for constituencies, partly proportional for regional lists. It is not pure PR but produces a more proportional result than FPTP alone.

❌ **Thinking proportional representation means every vote directly equals a seat**
PR means the overall distribution of seats reflects the overall share of votes. The exact mechanism varies by PR system (party lists, STV, etc.).

❌ **Forgetting that Northern Ireland uses a different PR system**
Scotland and Wales use the Additional Member System. Northern Ireland uses Single Transferable Vote (STV). They are different PR mechanisms — both produce proportional results but in different ways.

## Expert Tips

**1. Remember the key contrast for the test.** Westminster = First Past the Post. Devolved assemblies = proportional (or semi-proportional). That single distinction covers most test questions on this topic.

**2. Coalition governments are a clue.** PR systems tend to produce coalition governments because no single party usually wins a majority of seats. If a test question connects PR to coalition outcomes, that is likely correct.

**3. "More proportional" is enough.** The test does not ask you to explain STV or AMS in technical detail. It tests whether you understand the principle: more votes = more seats.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does the UK use proportional representation?

Not for general elections. UK general elections use First Past the Post. Proportional or semi-proportional systems are used in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland for devolved elections.

### What is the difference between First Past the Post and proportional representation?

Under First Past the Post, the candidate with the most votes in each constituency wins — regardless of whether they have a majority. Under PR, parties receive seats in proportion to their total share of votes across all constituencies.

### Where is proportional representation used in the UK?

The Scottish Parliament and Welsh Senedd use the Additional Member System. The Northern Ireland Assembly uses Single Transferable Vote. Both are proportional or semi-proportional systems.

### Why doesn't the UK use proportional representation for general elections?

The UK has used First Past the Post for general elections for centuries. A referendum on changing to the Alternative Vote system was held in 2011 and rejected. The debate about electoral reform continues, but FPTP remains the system for Westminster elections.

### What is Single Transferable Vote?

Single Transferable Vote (STV) is a form of proportional representation used in Northern Ireland. Voters rank candidates in order of preference. Seats are distributed to reflect those preferences proportionally.
