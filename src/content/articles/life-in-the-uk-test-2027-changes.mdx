## What the 2025 White Paper Actually Said

The UK government published the Immigration White Paper in May 2025. It proposed several significant changes to immigration law, including:

**A new Life in the UK test** focused on British values and democratic principles, rather than the current mix of history, culture, government, and society. Under the proposal, a new handbook would be written to replace the current 3rd edition. The questions on the current test — covering topics like specific historical dates, named inventors, and patron saints — would not appear on the new test.

**An extended ILR qualifying period** — the White Paper proposed increasing the time required to qualify for ILR from 5 years to 10 years on most routes. This would significantly delay the path to settlement for most migrants.

**Higher English language requirements** for some visa routes — the B2 English requirement for new Skilled Worker visa applications was a separate measure that came into force on 8 January 2026.

## What Has Actually Changed

As of June 2026, only one of these measures is in effect:

**Already in force:** B2 English for new Skilled Worker visa applications (from 8 January 2026). This applies to new grants of the Skilled Worker visa — not to people already on this route or to other visa categories.

**Not yet law:** The new Life in the UK test. No legislation has been passed. No implementation date has been set. The current test continues.

**Not yet law:** The 10-year ILR qualifying period. This remains a proposal.

## The Current Test Is Still the Test

The Life in the UK test you will sit today is the same test that has existed since April 2013 when the 3rd edition handbook was introduced. It consists of:

- 24 questions drawn from the official handbook (3rd edition)
- 45 minutes to complete the test
- 75% pass mark — you must answer at least 18 questions correctly
- Computer-based format at an approved test centre
- £50 for a weekday daytime slot, £75 for evenings and weekends

Nothing about this has changed. The official 3rd edition handbook — "Life in the United Kingdom: A Guide for New Residents" — is still the only source material. Every question on the test comes from this book, and only from this book.

## What Might the New Test Look Like?

The White Paper described a shift towards testing knowledge of British values and democratic principles. This is consistent with the approach taken by some other countries' citizenship tests — focusing on constitutional values, rights, and civic duties rather than historical trivia.

If a new test is introduced, it would require:
- A new official handbook to be written and published
- New test questions to be developed and validated
- A transition period, likely with advance notice to applicants

None of this has begun publicly as of June 2026.

## Will Existing Pass Certificates Still Be Valid?

This is the question most people preparing in 2026 are asking. The honest answer is: no official statement has been made .

In practice, when a test changes, governments typically honour existing pass certificates for a defined period. The Home Office did this when the 2nd edition handbook was replaced by the 3rd edition in 2013. However, until an official statement is made about any future change, there is no guarantee.

The safest position: if you need the test for an ILR or citizenship application, sit and pass the current test as soon as you are ready. A pass in hand removes all uncertainty.

## Other 2026 Immigration Changes

It is worth being clear about what else changed in 2026 that is separate from the Life in the UK test:

- **B2 English for Skilled Worker visas:** In force from 8 January 2026 for new visa grants. Does not change the Life in the UK test or its format.
- **eVisa system:** BRP cards were phased out in late 2024 and replaced by the eVisa (UKVI account-based digital status). This changes how you prove your immigration status but does not affect the test.
- **ILR 10-year rule:** Proposed in the White Paper. Not yet in force as of June 2026.
