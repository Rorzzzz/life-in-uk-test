## Introduction

British citizenship by descent is one of the least understood areas of UK nationality law. Many people discover they are already British — and have been for years — without knowing it. This article explains the rules clearly, including the one-generation limit and when you might be able to register as a British citizen. For a full comparison of citizenship routes, see our guide to [British citizenship requirements in 2026](/articles/british-citizenship-requirements-2026).

---

## What "British Citizenship by Descent" Means

UK nationality law uses two categories:

1. **British citizen otherwise than by descent** — someone who is British because they were born in the UK, or because they were registered or naturalised as British (and acquired a full, "transmittable" citizenship)
2. **British citizen by descent** — someone who is British because their parent was British, but they were not born in the UK

The distinction matters because only category 1 automatically transmits citizenship to the next generation born outside the UK.

---

## The One-Generation Rule

This is the most important rule to understand.

**Generation 1 (parent):** Born in the UK → British "otherwise than by descent"
**Generation 2 (you):** Born outside the UK to a British parent → British **by descent** (automatic)
**Generation 3 (your child):** Born outside the UK → NOT automatically British

The chain stops at one generation born outside the UK. Your child born abroad will not automatically get British citizenship through you — even though you are British.

**Example:**
- Your father was born in Manchester. He is British "otherwise than by descent."
- You were born in Australia to your British father. You are automatically British by descent.
- Your child is born in Australia. Your child is NOT automatically British.

---

## How to Know If Your Parent Was British "Otherwise Than by Descent"

Your parent was British "otherwise than by descent" if they:

- Were born in the UK after 1 January 1983 (when the British Nationality Act 1981 came into force), to a parent who was settled in the UK
- Were registered as a British citizen in the UK
- Were naturalised as a British citizen in the UK

If your parent was also British by descent (born abroad to a British grandparent), the rule is more complex and you may not automatically qualify. The law changed several times between 1948 and 1983 — older cases should be assessed by an immigration specialist.

---

## Do You Need to Apply to Be British by Descent?

No. If you qualify, you are already British. Citizenship by descent is automatic — it is not granted by an application.

What you may need to do is **prove** it. The standard way is to apply for a British passport. When you apply, you provide evidence of your parent's British citizenship (their birth certificate, their UK passport, or their naturalisation certificate). HM Passport Office then assesses whether you qualify.

There is no separate "citizenship by descent" certificate or registration process for people who are already British by descent.

---

## Registering as British: When You Are Not Automatically British

Some people born abroad to a British parent are NOT automatically British by descent — but they may be able to **register** as British citizens.

Registration is a discretionary process. It applies in certain situations, such as:

- A child born abroad to a British-by-descent parent, where the grandparent was born in the UK
- Children of British mothers born before 1961 (historic nationality law discriminated against mothers — some children were not recognised automatically)
- Children born stateless where one parent was British

The exact rules depend on your specific family history. The Home Office has multiple registration categories with different requirements.

**Child registration fee:** £1,012. Fees change annually — check gov.uk/register-british-citizen for the current amount before applying.
**Form to use:** Form UKF is used for most child registrations under Section 3(1) of the British Nationality Act 1981. Check gov.uk/register-british-citizen to confirm the correct form for your specific route.

---

## What British Citizenship by Descent Gives You

If you are a British citizen by descent, you have:

- The right to a British passport
- The right to live and work in the UK without a visa
- Access to the NHS
- The right to vote (once registered)
- UK consular protection abroad

You do **not** need ILR or any visa before coming to the UK. Citizenship by descent gives you full rights to enter and remain.

---

## What British Citizenship by Descent Does NOT Give You

Being British by descent comes with one key limitation compared to citizenship "otherwise than by descent":

**Your children born outside the UK will not automatically be British.**

If you are British by descent and your child is born abroad, they are not automatically British. You would need to register them, if a registration route exists — or they would need to come to the UK and eventually apply for settlement and then naturalisation.

There is an exception: if your child is born in the UK, they may automatically be British — depending on your immigration status at the time of their birth.

---

## How This Differs From Naturalisation

Naturalisation requires five years of lawful residence in the UK, ILR, and passing the Life in the UK test. See our guide on [how long it takes to get British citizenship](/articles/how-long-does-it-take-to-get-british-citizenship).

Citizenship by descent requires none of that. If you qualify, you are already British — regardless of whether you have ever lived in the UK.

If you are unsure whether you qualify by descent, it is worth checking before going through the naturalisation process. Some people spend five years qualifying for naturalisation when they were already British.

---

## Common Mistakes

❌ **Assuming citizenship passes automatically through multiple generations.** It does not. If you are British by descent (born outside the UK to a British parent), your own children born outside the UK are not automatically British. The chain breaks after one generation born abroad.

❌ **Thinking you need ILR before you can claim citizenship by descent.** You do not. Citizenship by descent is independent of residency. If you qualify, you can apply for a British passport without ever having lived in the UK.

❌ **Confusing "born to a British parent" with "automatically British."** Whether you are automatically British depends on the legal status of your parent's citizenship and when you were born. A parent who was British by descent cannot automatically pass that on to you if you were also born outside the UK.

❌ **Giving up because the rules seem complicated.** Many cases that look uncertain at first glance turn out to be straightforward. A one-hour consultation with a qualified immigration adviser can clarify your position — the cost is far less than a full naturalisation application.

---

## Expert Tips

1. **Find your parent's citizenship evidence first.** Before doing anything else, locate your parent's birth certificate, British passport, or naturalisation certificate. You will need this for any passport application or registration. Older documents may need to be obtained from the General Register Office or the Home Office.

2. **Check the date your parent became British.** The British Nationality Act 1981 came into force on 1 January 1983. Cases before that date follow earlier law (the British Nationality Act 1948 and others). If your parent became British before 1983, the rules are different — do not assume current rules apply.

3. **Do not apply for naturalisation if you might already be British.** Naturalisation costs £1,839 and is non-refundable. If there is any chance you are British by descent, check that first. It costs nothing to explore your eligibility before spending money on a different application.

4. **Check the [good character requirement](/articles/good-character-requirement-ilr-citizenship) if you are registering (not just claiming by descent).** Registration applications for citizenship are subject to a good character assessment. Automatic acquisition of citizenship by descent is not — but if you are applying to register, your character will be considered.

5. **Apply for a British passport in the country where you live.** If you are outside the UK and believe you are British by descent, you can apply for a British passport at a British embassy or consulate. You do not need to travel to the UK to apply.

## FAQs

### Am I British if my mother was British but my father was not?
Yes — provided your mother was British "otherwise than by descent" (for example, born in the UK) and you were born after the relevant law change. Historic nationality law did not always recognise mothers' citizenship — if you were born before 1983, the rules are more complex and you should check with an adviser.

### Can I pass British citizenship to my children if I am British by descent?
Not automatically. If your children are born outside the UK, they are not automatically British. You would need to investigate whether a registration route is available. If your children are born in the UK, the rules are different and they may be British automatically.

### Do I need to live in the UK to be British by descent?
No. British citizenship by descent does not require you to live in the UK. If you qualify, you are British regardless of where you live.

### What if my grandparent — not my parent — was British?
In most cases, citizenship does not pass automatically through grandparents. If your grandparent was British and your parent was also born outside the UK (making them British by descent), then you are not automatically British. There may be a registration route — this depends on your specific circumstances.

### How do I prove I am British by descent?
The standard route is to apply for a British passport. You provide evidence of your parent's British citizenship along with your birth certificate showing your relationship to that parent. HM Passport Office will assess your claim.

### Is British citizenship by descent the same as a right of abode?
No. Right of abode is a separate immigration status — it applies to some Commonwealth citizens. British citizenship by descent is a full British nationality status. The two are different in law.
