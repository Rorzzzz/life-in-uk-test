Indefinite Leave to Enter (ILE) and Indefinite Leave to Remain (ILR) are both forms of settled status in the UK. They give identical rights — the right to live and work in the UK permanently with no visa conditions. The difference is when they are granted: ILE is given at the UK border on arrival; ILR is granted by the Home Office while you are already in the UK.

Most people in the UK immigration system encounter ILR — not ILE. Understanding the difference is useful mainly because the names are confusingly similar.

## What Is ILE (Indefinite Leave to Enter)?

Indefinite Leave to Enter is a type of leave granted at the UK border — at a port of entry — when someone arrives with permission to settle in the UK.

ILE is stamped or recorded when:

- A person has been approved for settlement from outside the UK
- They enter the UK for the first time as a settler
- The leave is indefinite — it has no expiry date

The most common scenario where someone receives ILE rather than ILR: they had ILR previously, left the UK for a long period (but not so long that it lapsed), and returned to the UK. The border officer records their re-entry as ILE.

Another scenario: someone sponsored for immediate settlement who enters the UK for the first time on an entry visa that leads directly to settlement.

---

## What Is ILR (Indefinite Leave to Remain)?

Indefinite Leave to Remain is the in-country settlement status granted by the Home Office while you are already in the UK.

ILR is how most migrants settle in the UK. After 5 years on a qualifying visa, you apply to the Home Office for ILR. If approved, you are granted settled status from the date of the decision.

ILR is more common than ILE because most people who want to settle in the UK apply from inside the country after their qualifying period.

---

## Are the Rights Identical?

Yes. ILE and ILR grant exactly the same rights:

- Permanent right to live and work in the UK
- No visa conditions
- No sponsor required
- Access to public funds (NRPF removed)
- NHS as an ordinary resident
- Pathway to British citizenship (12 months after grant)

Neither ILE nor ILR is "better" than the other. The label simply reflects whether you were in or outside the UK when settlement was granted.

---

## The 2-Year Absence Rule — Both Apply

Both ILE and ILR lapse if you spend 2 or more continuous years outside the UK.

The rule applies regardless of which form of settlement you have. If you leave the UK for 2 or more years without returning, your settled status lapses — whether it is called ILE or ILR.

## Why Does the Distinction Matter?

In most practical situations, it does not matter whether your settled status is called ILE or ILR.

**Where it can matter:**

- **Citizenship applications:** Both ILE and ILR give the same 12-month wait before applying for naturalisation. The citizenship application form asks for the type of leave — enter whichever applies.
- **Historical records:** People who entered the UK decades ago may have ILE stamped in their passport rather than a newer ILR grant. Both are valid forms of settlement. Older ILE stamps are still recognised.
- **Returning residents:** If you had ILR, left the UK for an extended period (but less than 2 years), and returned, you may have been re-admitted as ILE rather than ILR. Your settled status is unchanged.

## Common Mistakes

❌ **Thinking ILE is a lesser form of settlement than ILR**
They are equal. Both are settled status. Both give the same rights. Both lead to citizenship on the same timeline. Neither is better.

❌ **Worrying that an ILE stamp in an old passport is invalid**
An older ILE stamp is still valid evidence of settled status, provided it has not lapsed. If the settlement is still active (continuous UK residence maintained), the ILE grants the same rights as ILR.

❌ **Not applying the 2-year absence rule to ILE**
Some people assume ILE is permanent in a way that ILR is not. Both lapse after 2 continuous years outside the UK. The rule applies equally.

❌ **Confusing ILE with an entry visa**
ILE is not a temporary entry visa. It is a permanent settlement status granted at the point of entry. The word "enter" refers to when it was granted — not that you need to "enter" repeatedly.

## Expert Tips

**1. For citizenship applications, use whichever label appears on your evidence.** If your eVisa shows ILE, write ILE. If it shows ILR, write ILR. Both are valid answers. The citizenship rules treat them identically.

**2. If you received ILE on a historical passport stamp, log in to your UKVI account to confirm your current eVisa status.** Old stamps are valid — but it is worth confirming your current digital status reflects active settlement.

**3. The 12-month citizenship clock runs from the date of the ILE or ILR grant.** Whether your settlement is called ILE or ILR, the 12-month wait to apply for citizenship begins on the date it was granted.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between ILE and ILR?

ILE (Indefinite Leave to Enter) is settlement granted at the UK border when you arrive. ILR (Indefinite Leave to Remain) is settlement granted by the Home Office while you are already in the UK. Both give identical rights — permanent residence with no visa conditions.

### Which is better — ILE or ILR?

Neither. They are equal. Both grant settled status with the same rights, the same path to citizenship, and the same 2-year absence rule. The name reflects where and how the settlement was granted, not the quality of the status.

### Does ILE count toward British citizenship?

Yes. ILE gives you the same 12-month wait before applying for naturalisation as a British citizen. It counts in exactly the same way as ILR.

### Does the 2-year absence rule apply to ILE?

Yes. ILE lapses after 2 or more continuous years outside the UK — the same rule as ILR.

### Do I have ILE or ILR?

Check your UKVI account or eVisa. For older cases, check any Home Office grant letters or passport stamps. If you applied for settlement from inside the UK, it will typically be ILR. If your settlement was granted when you arrived at a UK border, it will typically be ILE.

## How This Aligns With Official Guidance

Both ILE and ILR are forms of leave under the Immigration Act 1971. The rights they confer, the 2-year absence rule, and the pathway to British citizenship are identical. The distinction is set out in the Immigration Rules and Home Office guidance on settlement.
