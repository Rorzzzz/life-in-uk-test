General elections use First Past the Post voting. The monarch is Head of State but does not govern — the Prime Minister leads the government. ## Parliament: Two Chambers

The UK Parliament sits at Westminster in London. It has two chambers:

**The House of Commons** is the elected chamber. It has **650 Members of Parliament (MPs)**. Each MP represents one constituency. Voters in each constituency elect one MP at a general election. The party that wins the most seats usually forms the government.

**The House of Lords** is not elected. Members are appointed — either as life peers nominated by the Prime Minister, or as bishops of the Church of England. There are also a small number of hereditary peers. The Lords can delay and suggest changes to laws, but they cannot permanently block a law passed by the Commons.

The test frequently asks whether the Lords is elected. The answer is **no**.

---

## How Parliament Makes Laws

A new law begins as a Bill. It passes through several readings and debates in both the Commons and the Lords. Once both chambers agree on the text, the monarch gives Royal Assent and the Bill becomes an Act of Parliament — a law.

The monarch has not refused Royal Assent since 1707. In practice, Royal Assent is a formality.

---

## General Elections and Voting

General elections must be held at least every **5 years**. The Prime Minister can call an earlier election under certain conditions.

**The voting system for UK general elections is First Past the Post (FPTP).** In each constituency, the candidate with the most votes wins — even if they do not have a majority. There is no second round, no proportional allocation. The party that wins the most seats in the Commons forms the government.

**Voting age:** You must be **18** to vote in UK general elections. In Scotland and Wales, the voting age is **16** for elections to the Scottish Parliament and Welsh Senedd.

You must be registered to vote. EU citizens living in the UK can vote in local elections but not UK general elections. Commonwealth citizens living in the UK can vote in UK general elections.

## The Role of the Monarch

The monarch is the **Head of State**. The current monarch is King Charles III. The monarch's role is constitutional — ceremonial and formal.

The monarch does **not** govern. The monarch does not make laws, set policy, or lead the military in practice. These powers belong to elected politicians.

The monarch's formal duties include:
- Opening Parliament each year (the King's Speech)
- Giving Royal Assent to Bills passed by Parliament
- Appointing the Prime Minister (by convention, the leader of the largest party)
- Representing the UK on state visits abroad

## The Prime Minister and the Cabinet

The **Prime Minister** is the head of the government. By convention, the Prime Minister is the leader of the party that wins the most seats in a general election.

The Prime Minister appoints the **Cabinet** — senior ministers who lead government departments such as health, education, and defence. The Cabinet meets regularly to make collective decisions on government policy.

## Devolved Governments

The UK has devolved some powers to separate assemblies in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. These bodies can make their own laws in certain areas.

### Scottish Parliament
Located in Edinburgh. The Scottish Parliament was created in 1999. It uses a form of proportional representation (Additional Member System). It has powers over health, education, justice, and some tax rates in Scotland.

### Welsh Senedd (Welsh Parliament)
Located in Cardiff. Also created in 1999. Originally had limited powers — these have expanded over time. The Senedd has powers over health, education, and local government in Wales.

### Northern Ireland Assembly
Located at Stormont in Belfast. Created under the Good Friday Agreement in 1998. Has powers over health, education, and agriculture in Northern Ireland.

### What Stays at Westminster (Reserved Powers)

The following areas are **not devolved** and remain under Westminster control:
- Defence and the armed forces
- Immigration and nationality
- Foreign policy and international relations
- Social security (benefits) — partially
- Broadcasting

This is a key test area. Know which powers are devolved and which are reserved.

## The Legal Systems of the UK

The UK does not have a single legal system. There are three:

1. **England and Wales** — one legal system
2. **Scotland** — a separate legal system (based partly on Roman law)
3. **Northern Ireland** — its own legal system

**Criminal law:** The state prosecutes people who break criminal law. Examples include theft, assault, and fraud.

**Civil law:** Disputes between individuals or organisations. Examples include contract disputes, personal injury claims, and employment disputes.

---

## The Courts

**Magistrates courts** deal with minor criminal cases. Magistrates (also called Justices of the Peace) hear cases without a jury. They can impose fines and short prison sentences.

**Crown Court** handles serious criminal cases, such as murder, robbery, and rape. These cases are heard by a judge and a **jury of 12 members of the public**.

**The Supreme Court** is the highest court in the UK. It hears appeals on points of law of general public importance.

**Small claims court** handles civil disputes involving smaller amounts of money. £10,000 in England and Wales

---

## Jury Service

Adults on the **electoral register** between the ages of **18 and 75 may be called for jury service. Jury service is a legal obligation. You can be fined for failing to attend without good reason.

A jury has **12 members** in Crown Court cases in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland. In Scotland, a jury has **15 members**.

## Your Rights and Responsibilities

### The Equality Act 2010
The Equality Act protects people from discrimination based on **protected characteristics**. These include:

- Age
- Disability
- Gender reassignment
- Marriage and civil partnership
- Pregnancy and maternity
- Race
- Religion or belief
- Sex
- Sexual orientation

Discrimination can be direct (treating someone worse because of a characteristic) or indirect (applying a rule that disadvantages a group).

### The Human Rights Act 1998
The Human Rights Act incorporates the **European Convention on Human Rights** into UK law. It allows people to bring human rights cases in UK courts without going to the European Court in Strasbourg.

Key rights protected include: the right to life, freedom from torture, the right to a fair trial, freedom of thought and expression, and the right to respect for private and family life.

## Common Mistakes

❌ **Saying the House of Lords is elected** — It is not. Members are appointed. The test includes questions designed to catch this misunderstanding.

❌ **Confusing the monarch's role with the Prime Minister's** — The monarch is Head of State but does not govern. The Prime Minister heads the government. These are different roles.

❌ **Getting devolved powers wrong** — Defence, immigration, and foreign policy stay at Westminster. Health, education, and housing are devolved — but the exact powers differ between Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

❌ **Mixing up the voting age rules** — 18 for UK general elections. 16 for Scottish Parliament and Welsh Senedd elections. A test question may present both and ask you to distinguish them.

❌ **Assuming the UK has one legal system** — Scotland has a distinct legal system from England and Wales. Northern Ireland has its own as well.

## Expert Tips

1. **Learn the devolved powers as a list.** Write out: health ✅ education ✅ defence ❌ immigration ❌ foreign policy ❌. Test yourself until you can recall this instantly — it appears in many question variants.

2. **Associate 650 with the Commons and "appointed" with the Lords.** These two facts appear repeatedly in different question formats. If the question mentions elections, the answer relates to the Commons. If it mentions appointed members, it relates to the Lords.

3. **Remember the legal system split as England+Wales / Scotland / Northern Ireland.** The test asks about this in multiple ways — which courts exist, which systems differ, and which laws apply where.

4. **Connect the Equality Act (2010) and Human Rights Act (1998) to their dates.** The test sometimes asks which Act was passed in which year. 2010 for Equality, 1998 for Human Rights.

## FAQs

### How many questions in the Life in the UK test come from Chapter 5?

Around 4–5 questions in a 24-question test come from Chapter 5, making it approximately 20% of the paper. The exact number varies between sittings. No chapter is guaranteed a fixed number of questions.

### Is the House of Lords elected?

No. Members of the House of Lords are appointed — either as life peers, bishops of the Church of England, or (in smaller numbers) hereditary peers. The House of Lords is never elected by the public.

### What powers do the Scottish Parliament, Welsh Senedd, and Northern Ireland Assembly have?

They have devolved powers over areas such as health, education, and housing. Defence, immigration, and foreign policy remain reserved to the Westminster Parliament and are not devolved to any of these bodies.

### What voting system is used in UK general elections?

UK general elections use First Past the Post (FPTP). The candidate with the most votes in each constituency wins that seat. The party winning the most seats usually forms the government.

### What is the voting age in the UK?

You must be 18 to vote in UK general elections and local elections in England. In Scotland and Wales, the voting age is 16 for elections to the Scottish Parliament and Welsh Senedd.

### What does the Equality Act 2010 protect?

The Equality Act 2010 protects people from discrimination based on protected characteristics. These include age, disability, race, sex, sexual orientation, religion or belief, pregnancy and maternity, gender reassignment, and marriage and civil partnership.

### What is the Human Rights Act 1998?

The Human Rights Act 1998 brought the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law. It lets people bring human rights cases in UK courts rather than going to the European Court of Human Rights in Strasbourg.
